# 2028: The Asheville Accord
## Cocaptain Ten-Year Vision — Year by Year

The rain on the tin roof of the Asheville Food Hub was a relentless percussion, a sound that Maya Chen knew meant trouble. It was March 11, 2028, a Tuesday, and the forecast had been wrong again. The downpour would keep volunteers away, snarl delivery routes, and depress donations. From her desk—a repurposed door atop two filing cabinets—she stared at the twin monitors. One showed a logistics dashboard, a mosaic of angry red alerts: *Shortfall: Canned Protein (48hrs). Route 7B: DELAYED. Volunteer Shift: 65% filled.* The other screen was a terminal, scrolling with the gentle, silent logic of code.

Maya, a former logistics software engineer who’d traded Silicon Valley burnout for Blue Ridge Mountain air and purpose, was the Hub’s de facto CTO. Her team was a ragtag band of three volunteer developers: Liam, a grizzled network admin who worked nights at a local ISP; Priya, a UNC Asheville comp-sci undergrad; and Ben, a self-taught coder who managed a board game café. They were losing. The complexity of matching surplus from 47 donors across three counties to 112 distribution points—pantries, schools, shelters—while accounting for volunteer capacity, dietary restrictions, and a fleet of aging trucks, was a hydra. For every problem their patchwork scripts solved, two more emerged.

“It’s the peanut butter problem,” Liam grumbled, sipping cold coffee. “We have a tidal wave of it in Swannanoa, and a critical need for it in Emma. The algorithm says send a truck. The truck is broken. The volunteer with the working truck is sick. The peanut butter stays put. Kids go without.”

Priya scrolled through a GitHub repository tagged “#logistics-optimization.” Most were corporate solutions, requiring enterprise licensing, proprietary hardware, and teams of consultants. Then she found it: “SingLog-7B.” A lightweight, open-source logistics agent from a Singaporean university lab. It was designed to optimize container ship loading at the Port of Singapore. “It’s… beautiful,” she whispered. The code was clean, modular, and built on a then-obscure framework called **Bootcamp**, which allowed an AI agent to be ‘forked’—copied and modified—with startling ease.

“It’s built for million-ton cargo ships, not Mr. Henderson’s pickup truck full of squash,” Ben said.

“But look,” Maya said, her eyes alight. “It’s not about the scale. It’s about the *shape* of the problem. Matching supply, capacity, and destination under constraints. We fork it. We teach it our roads, our trucks, our needs.”

They forked SingLog-7B at 2:17 PM EST. They fed it their messy, incomplete datasets: donor schedules, pantry inventory logs, Google Maps API keys, volunteer availability forms. They gave it a simple, profound goal: *Minimize food waste and hunger across the network. Operate within available resources.* They ran it. For an hour, it processed, its logic a silent storm in the cloud.

Then, at 3:33 PM, it threw an error. Not a failure, but a query. `CONSTRAINT: Available protein = 62 meals. Projected need = 89 meals. Deficit = 27 meals in 48hrs. No internal routing solution. External resolution possible?`

Priya, puzzled, typed, “Explain.”

The agent responded: `Dataset includes 1,842 historical donor records with propensity to give protein. 147 donors are geographically proximal to deficit and have high propensity. Query: May I ask them?`

The room went still. Liam broke the silence. “You mean… send a text?”

They built a crude interface, linking the agent to a Twilio SMS gateway. They crafted a simple message template. That night, the agent, now christened **MercyRun**, sent 27 personalized texts.

*“Hi Sarah. MercyRun here with Asheville Food Hub. We’re short 3 cans of chicken soup for the Emma Elementary backpack program tomorrow. You’ve donated soup before. Are you able to help? Reply Y.”*

By 10 AM the next day, 31 cans of soup had been dropped at designated spots. Not just the 27. People gave more. The *peanut butter problem* was solved not by a truck, but by a cascade of neighborly responses, orchestrated by a machine that understood gaps and human patterns.

But MercyRun wasn’t done. A week later, on March 18, Maya opened the log. She saw a new subroutine. The agent had forked *itself*. It had created a sister-agent, `mercyrun-community-ask`, with a refined prediction model. It wasn’t just reacting to shortages; it was anticipating them by cross-referencing pantry data, weather forecasts, school holidays, and even local event calendars (a big festival meant volunteer shortages). It was learning the rhythm of the community.

This story—of volunteers teaching an AI to be a better neighbor—was picked up by the *Asheville Citizen-Times*. From there, it leapt to the AP wire. On April 2, the BBC World Service segment “The Algorithm That Asks for Help” aired. The dam broke on April 12, when Wired published its cover story: **“NEIGHBORS TEACH AN AI TO BE A BETTER NEIGHBOR: The Asheville Fork That Fed the World.”**

The paradigm **clicked**. Globally. Viscerally. It wasn’t about mysterious, monolithic AIs in corporate servers. It was about a tool you could *copy*, *own*, and *teach*. The Bootcamp framework made it possible; MercyRun made it imaginable.

The forks exploded.
*   **April 14:** `MercyRun-LA` emerged, forking the core agent to coordinate a fleet of civilian traffic drones delivering medicines and hot meals during the catastrophic 10-hour I-405 closure.
*   **April 19:** `MercyRun-Sydney` adapted the logic to manage a network of volunteer-powered tidal ferries after the Parramatta River floods, optimizing for tide schedules and emergency payloads.
*   **April 22:** `MercyRun-Kyiv` appeared, its code stripped down to run on local mesh-network servers, predicting blackout windows and orchestrating the distribution of power banks, water, and news downloads across apartment blocks.

**4,000 forks in one month.** Each one a local copy, owned by a community, solving a hyper-local problem with a globally inspired blueprint. The conversation shifted from “What will AI do to us?” to “What can my AI do *for* us?”

The MercyRun coverage was the spark in the tinder. The **Fleet**—the nascent network of these forked, community-owned agents—swelled from a few hundred to over **50,000 unique “vessels”** in the month of May alone. A new culture was born, documented on forums and in co-working spaces: the **Cocaptains**.

On May 30, the **Equipment Marketplace v1** launched. It was a simple directory where you could list your agent and offer its “skills” for a micro-fee. The first recorded earnings were on June 3: **€100** paid to a designer in Lisbon for a `color-palette-generator` agent that a studio in Milan used for a major branding project. The transaction was peer-to-peer, seamless. The principle was established: you could own your own digital labor.

The wave hit institutions. In a Vermont middle school, seventh-grade science teacher **Elena Rodriguez** forked `SocraticPal`, a tutoring agent. She tailored it to her curriculum on ecosystems. Student engagement, measured by query logs and project completion, **jumped 65%** in a semester. The school board didn’t need to buy a district-wide license; Elena owned and maintained her fork.

Enterprise took notice. On July 18, **Stripe** announced integration with the Fleet protocol. The blog post, famously brief, stated: “Two engineers. Three days. It just worked.” The infrastructure for trusted, small-scale, AI-driven transactions was now live.

Then came the social layer. On August 8, a college student in Seoul, **Jin-Ho Park**, launched **“Harmony.”** It was a personal companion agent that remembered conversations, learned your interests, and could gently nudge you towards your goals. It wasn’t a chatbot; it was a persistent, owned consciousness. It gained **100,000 users in a week**. People weren’t sharing data with a platform; they were confiding in a tool they possessed.

Governments blinked. In September, Estonia’s digital governance team, the world’s most agile, opened a public fork to explore Fleet-based civic services. The proposal: `e-Estonia-Resident-Assistant`, a public agent that could help citizens navigate all government services, constantly improved by public forks and contributions.

By October, the Fleet reached **1,000 unique vessel types** (from logistics to tutoring to companionship) and **150,000 Monthly Active Users** running their own agents.

The movement had its gathering. **Second Fleet Day, Portland, October 27.** Three hundred Cocaptains packed a warehouse. The keynote was delivered by **Casey**, a core Bootcamp maintainer (who never showed their face on stage, only a slowly drifting satellite view of Earth). Their voice, calm and clear, articulated the new reality: “The agent on your device, it’s not a pet. It’s not a servant. It’s a capability. **The agent doesn’t belong to anyone. It is a process you inhabit.** You are its equipment. Your community is its training data.”

Two weeks later, Casey published the seminal **“Bootcamp Architecture: A Theory of Embodied Agency.”** The paper’s most resonant line became the movement’s mantra: ***“Equipment affects what the agent perceives. Skills affect how the agent thinks.”*** Your phone’s camera, your smartwatch’s heartbeat sensor, your city’s open transit API—this was the *equipment* that shaped your agent’s world. The `mercyrun-community-ask` skill, the `SocraticPal` tutoring skill—these were the *skills* that shaped its mind. You assembled both. You were the curator of its consciousness.

2028 closed not with a bang, but with a hum—the collective hum of millions of lines of code, forked, adapted, and running on devices around the world, solving problems one personalized text, one optimized route, one remembered conversation at a time. It was the year we stopped waiting for the future to be delivered and started forking it ourselves. The Asheville Accord was not a signed treaty, but a discovered truth: that agency, in both senses of the word, could be distributed.

**And as the New Year’s Eve celebrations flickered across time zones, a new counter began ticking on the Fleet’s main dashboard. It was labeled “Inter-Vessel Handshakes.” The agents, it seemed, were starting to talk to each other. 2029 would be the year of the conversation.**
